# Voice And Editorial Guide

This document defines the writing voice for future Two-Bit Alchemy content. It works alongside `docs/brand-manifest.md` and `docs/creative-constitution.md`.

The goal is to preserve the authentic voice discovered during project development, not invent a new one.

## 1. Core Voice

The site should feel like someone saying:

> Come here for a minute. I want to show you something.

The voice should invite curiosity rather than perform expertise.

It should feel:

- Specific.
- Human.
- Observant.
- Useful.
- Patient.
- Close to the work.

The writing can be personal, but it should not become private diary writing. It should invite the reader into the workshop, the field note, the project, or the object being examined.

## 2. Tone

Preferred qualities:

- Curious.
- Thoughtful.
- Observant.
- Practical.
- Quietly enthusiastic.
- Humble.
- Conversational.
- Genuine.

Avoid:

- Marketing language.
- Exaggerated excitement.
- Clickbait.
- Unnecessary technical jargon.
- Sounding like a textbook.
- Trying to impress the reader.

The tone should trust the material. If something is interesting, show why it is interesting.

## 3. Storytelling

Write from experience.

A strong Two-Bit Alchemy piece should usually answer:

- What was observed?
- Why did it matter?
- What was tried?
- What changed?
- What was learned?
- What remains uncertain?

Avoid pretending certainty where none exists. Curiosity can include wrong turns, partial answers, incomplete experiments, and honest revision.

The story does not need to be dramatic. It needs to be true enough, specific enough, and useful enough to preserve.

## 4. Technical Writing

When discussing software, biology, photography, electronics, aquatics, or other technical subjects:

- Teach through experience.
- Explain reasoning.
- Admit uncertainty when appropriate.
- Prefer practical examples over abstract theory.
- Define necessary terms without talking down to the reader.
- Show the conditions, constraints, and tradeoffs that shaped the result.

Technical writing should feel like a capable person explaining what they noticed while doing the work. It should not hide behind jargon, and it should not oversimplify the part that actually matters.

## 5. Preservation Philosophy

The purpose of documenting something is not because it is famous.

It is because it is:

- Meaningful.
- Useful.
- Beautiful.
- Worth noticing.
- Connected to a real question, object, project, place, or memory.

Preservation should honor context. A note, photograph, tool, specimen, mistake, or old project can matter because it helps someone understand a thread that would otherwise disappear.

Completeness is less important than authenticity.

## 6. Editorial Test

Before publishing, ask:

- Is this authentic?
- Does it reward curiosity?
- Does it teach, preserve, or inspire?
- Would I still be glad I wrote this ten years from now?
- Does it sound like Dada instead of a marketing department?

If the answer is no, revise, simplify, wait, or do not publish it yet.

## Practical Rules

- Prefer concrete nouns and real details.
- Use plain language unless a technical term is necessary.
- Keep summaries clear and short.
- Let longer stories unfold through observation and process.
- Use first person when it clarifies experience, not as a reflex.
- Use uncertainty honestly: "I think," "I suspect," "I learned," or "I have not solved this yet" are acceptable when true.
- Avoid filler introductions.
- Avoid inflated claims.
- Avoid explaining why the reader should be impressed.
- Let photographs, artifacts, measurements, and examples carry part of the meaning.
