# Shelf Worthiness

This guide defines the philosophy used to decide whether an object, story, photograph, project, memory, or exhibit belongs in the permanent collection of Two-Bit Alchemy.

It is documentation only. It does not modify the website, theme, templates, WordPress configuration, or live content.

## Purpose

Shelf Worthiness helps decide what earns a lasting place in Two-Bit Alchemy.

"Earning a shelf" is not about monetary value, rarity, prestige, fame, or polish. It is about meaning, memory, usefulness, kindness, curiosity, and the ability to preserve something that would otherwise be easy to lose.

The shelf is not infinite. It should be generous, but not indiscriminate.

## Guiding Philosophy

Two-Bit Alchemy preserves the things that reward curiosity.

Some things belong because they taught something. Some belong because they carry a story. Some belong because they preserve an act of kindness, a moment of survival, a strange connection, a project history, or a trace of how Dada came to see the world.

Shelf Worthiness asks whether an item helps preserve the person behind the work.

The question is not:

- Is it valuable?
- Is it rare?
- Is it impressive?
- Would someone else collect it?

The question is:

- Does it explain something worth preserving?

## Shelf Worthiness Questions

Use these questions when deciding whether something belongs in the permanent collection:

- Did it teach something?
- Did it preserve an act of kindness?
- Did it change how I see the world?
- Does it preserve a story worth losing?
- Does it help explain the person behind Two-Bit Alchemy?
- Would future family members understand me better because it exists?
- Does it connect to a real project, place, person, question, or memory?
- Does it show process, survival, curiosity, care, repair, failure, discovery, or attention?
- Would its absence make the archive less true?
- Can it be preserved with enough context to matter to someone else later?

If the answer to several of these questions is yes, the item may have earned a shelf.

## Examples

Generic examples of shelf-worthy material:

- A common object connected to a specific act of kindness.
- A damaged tool that explains how a project was made.
- A photograph that preserves a place, person, or observation that would otherwise fade.
- A notebook page that shows how an idea changed over time.
- A small model, ticket, label, or scrap that carries a larger memory.
- A failed experiment that taught something useful.
- A family object that helps future relatives understand a person more clearly.

Generic examples that may not earn a shelf:

- An object kept only because it is mildly interesting.
- A photograph with no story, context, or useful connection.
- A souvenir whose meaning has been lost.
- A project fragment that no longer teaches, explains, preserves, or connects.
- Material that is private, sensitive, or unresolved and should not be made public.

Not every interesting thing earns a shelf.

## Permanent Collection Boundaries

Not every shelf item becomes an exhibit.

Some objects may remain:

- Private.
- Family-only.
- Workbench-only.
- Reference material.
- A note attached to another exhibit.
- A future possibility waiting for context.

Some stories remain private.

Some stories stay in the Workbench indefinitely.

The Workbench can preserve something without forcing it into public view.

## Relationship To The Creative Constitution

`docs/creative-constitution.md` says Two-Bit Alchemy exists to collect, preserve, and share the things that reward curiosity.

Shelf Worthiness helps decide what belongs in that collection. It protects the site from becoming either too sparse or too cluttered by asking whether an item preserves real curiosity, memory, learning, kindness, or context.

It also supports the Creative Constitution's rule that authenticity is more important than completeness.

## Relationship To The Editorial Workflow

`docs/editorial-workflow.md` describes how raw material moves from conversation to Workbench notes, editorial draft, fact review, final exhibit, and publication.

Shelf Worthiness belongs early in that process. It helps decide whether a raw object or memory should:

- Stay in conversation.
- Move into the Workbench.
- Become an artifact draft.
- Become part of a larger exhibit.
- Remain private.
- Wait indefinitely.

Editorial work should not force shelf-worthiness into material that does not have it.

## Relationship To The Exhibit Standard

`docs/exhibit-standard.md` defines what makes an exhibit ready for publication.

Shelf Worthiness comes before exhibit readiness. An item may be shelf-worthy but not exhibit-ready.

An exhibit asks:

- Is this ready to be understood by a visitor?

Shelf Worthiness asks:

- Does this deserve to be preserved at all?

Both standards matter. A shelf-worthy item still needs context, review, accessibility, image handling, and publication readiness before it becomes a public exhibit.

## Relationship To The Workbench

`content/workbench/README.md` defines the Workbench as the place where new material begins life.

The Workbench is allowed to hold uncertain, incomplete, private, or unpolished material. Shelf Worthiness can be discovered there slowly.

An object may enter the Workbench because it might matter. It earns a shelf only after its meaning becomes clearer.

The Workbench protects early material from being lost. Shelf Worthiness protects the permanent collection from becoming cluttered or false.

## Decision Rule

When deciding whether something earns a shelf, ask:

Does this help preserve a true thread of curiosity, memory, kindness, learning, or identity?

If yes, keep working with it.

If no, let it remain a note, a private memory, or nothing at all.
