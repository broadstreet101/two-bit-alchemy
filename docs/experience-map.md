# Experience Map

This document describes the intended visitor journey through Two-Bit Alchemy.

It is a planning document only. It does not modify the theme, templates, CSS, content model, or WordPress configuration.

## 1. Purpose Of The Experience

The Two-Bit Alchemy experience should help visitors follow curiosity.

The site should feel like being invited into a thoughtful workshop, field notebook, and cabinet of curiosities. Visitors should be able to arrive through any doorway, understand where they are, and find another meaningful thread to follow.

The experience should:

- Reward close attention.
- Preserve real projects, observations, artifacts, and discoveries.
- Encourage wandering without making navigation confusing.
- Help visitors understand the relationship between current work and durable knowledge.
- Keep personal context available without turning the site into a resume or private journal.

The goal is not to push every visitor toward the same destination. The goal is to make each path feel intentional, human, and worth following.

## 2. First-Time Visitor Path

A first-time visitor should quickly understand that Two-Bit Alchemy is not a portfolio, sales site, or ordinary blog.

Recommended first-time path:

1. Home introduces the identity, the Welcome, and the guiding rhythm: Observe. Record. Understand. Create.
2. Featured Projects offer concrete entry points into real work.
3. Field Notes and Workshop Journal clarify that the site contains both durable knowledge and active process.
4. Cabinet suggests that objects, references, tools, and artifacts are part of the experience.
5. About provides personal context without requiring the visitor to read it first.
6. Contact remains available but quiet.

First-time visitors should always have a clear next step, but the site should not over-explain itself. The invitation should feel like: "Come here for a minute. I want to show you something."

## 3. Returning Visitor Path

A returning visitor is more likely to ask, "What has Dada been curious about lately?"

Recommended returning path:

- Check the homepage for recent Workshop Journal entries or surfaced project activity.
- Visit Workshop Journal for process, experiments, repairs, failures, and active work.
- Visit Field Notes when looking for durable explanations, observations, or methods.
- Revisit Projects to see how long-running work is developing.
- Wander through Cabinet when looking for objects, references, photographs, or supporting context.

Returning visitors should be rewarded with continuity. New material should connect back to earlier projects, notes, objects, and questions when those relationships are real.

## 4. Natural Content Relationships

Each major section should feel distinct, but no section should feel isolated.

Natural relationships:

- Projects connect to Workshop Journal for process updates.
- Projects connect to Field Notes for durable lessons or reference material.
- Projects connect to Cabinet for objects, tools, photographs, references, and artifacts.
- Workshop Journal entries may later produce Field Notes when a process insight becomes evergreen.
- Field Notes may reference Projects when knowledge came from specific work.
- Cabinet entries should support Projects, Field Notes, Workshop Journal, and Photography through source material and object context.
- About should orient visitors toward the site's purpose, philosophy, Projects, and Contact.
- Photography can exist as a Project and also support other sections as evidence.

Relationships should be based on real relevance, not automatic linking.

## 5. Cross-Linking Philosophy

Cross-links should help visitors follow a thread.

Use cross-links when they:

- Clarify where an idea came from.
- Show how a project developed.
- Connect process to durable knowledge.
- Provide source, artifact, or visual context.
- Help visitors continue exploring without forcing a single path.

Avoid cross-links when they:

- Exist only for search optimization.
- Interrupt reading without adding meaning.
- Point to weakly related content.
- Turn every page into a dense web of choices.
- Make the site feel like a funnel.

Good cross-linking should feel like labeled drawers in a workshop: available, useful, and close to the thing being discussed.

## 6. Suggested "You May Also Enjoy..." Relationships

"You may also enjoy..." sections should be used sparingly and only when the relationship is meaningful.

Suggested relationships:

- Project page -> related Workshop Journal entries showing process.
- Project page -> related Field Notes explaining methods, observations, or lessons.
- Project page -> related Cabinet items such as tools, references, artifacts, or photographs.
- Workshop Journal entry -> parent Project when the work belongs to a larger project.
- Workshop Journal entry -> Field Note when the entry produced reusable knowledge.
- Field Note -> related Project when the note came from practical work.
- Field Note -> related Cabinet item when an object, book, specimen, or reference supports the note.
- Cabinet item -> related Project, Field Note, or Workshop Journal entry that gives it context.
- Photography page or gallery -> related Project, Field Note, or Cabinet object when photographs document a real thread.
- About -> Projects, Philosophy context, and Contact.

The label should remain quiet and conversational. It should suggest continuation, not demand engagement.

## 7. Section Reinforcement

### Projects

Projects are the durable bodies of work. They give visitors a place to understand history, purpose, progress, and outcomes.

Projects are reinforced by:

- Workshop Journal entries for process.
- Field Notes for evergreen lessons.
- Cabinet items for supporting artifacts and references.
- Photography when images document or clarify the work.

### Field Notes

Field Notes preserve knowledge that should remain useful beyond the day it was written.

Field Notes are reinforced by:

- Projects that generated the observation.
- Workshop Journal entries that show how the note emerged.
- Cabinet items that provide sources, tools, or artifacts.
- Photography that functions as evidence.

### Workshop Journal

Workshop Journal shows active curiosity and process over time.

Workshop Journal is reinforced by:

- Projects that give the work a larger home.
- Field Notes that preserve lessons after the process settles.
- Cabinet items that document tools, materials, references, or artifacts.

### Cabinet

Cabinet gathers the physical, visual, and reference material that supports curiosity.

Cabinet is reinforced by:

- Projects that give objects purpose.
- Field Notes that explain meaning or use.
- Workshop Journal entries that show objects in process.
- Visual Language principles that treat artifacts as evidence rather than decoration.

### About

About gives visitors enough personal and philosophical context to understand the site.

About is reinforced by:

- Projects as proof of curiosity through making.
- Field Notes as proof of observation and preservation.
- Workshop Journal as proof of process.
- Cabinet as proof that objects, references, and memory matter.

## 8. Encouraging Curiosity Without Overwhelming Visitors

The site should encourage wandering without making every page feel overstuffed.

Principles:

- Offer one or two strong next steps rather than many weak links.
- Keep section introductions short and concrete.
- Use reading width for explanation and wide width only when comparison or browsing benefits.
- Let titles, excerpts, captions, and metadata do orientation work.
- Prefer meaningful empty states over fake completeness.
- Avoid adding a new section when existing sections can hold the content clearly.
- Do not force visitors to understand the whole archive before enjoying one piece of it.
- Preserve calm spacing so related items have room to be noticed.

Curiosity should be invited by specificity, not volume.

## 9. Future Area Integration

Future areas should enter the experience only when they have enough real content and governance to support them.

### Tributes / Memorials

Tributes and Memorials should integrate as a meaningful personal archive, not as generic blog content.

Natural relationships:

- About for personal context.
- Cabinet for letters, photographs, objects, documents, and artifacts.
- Photography when images preserve memory or place.

Requirements before implementation:

- Privacy review.
- Permission rules.
- Submission expectations if stories or photos from others are accepted.
- Respectful editorial tone.

### Clutch Archive / Interactive References

The Clutch Archive should integrate as a future special Project.

Natural relationships:

- Projects for the main archive or restoration work.
- Field Notes for reference explanations and research.
- Cabinet for preserved materials, screenshots, flyers, links, or source artifacts.
- Workshop Journal for restoration process or technical notes.

Requirements before implementation:

- Copyright review.
- Attribution rules.
- Source tracking.
- Clear limits around lyrics, archival material, images, and restored website content.

### Memorials As Part Of The Larger Experience

Memorial content should support the site's preservation purpose while remaining careful and human.

It should feel connected to memory, artifacts, photographs, stories, and personal history. It should not be treated as filler, generic tribute content, or a separate emotional mode disconnected from the rest of Two-Bit Alchemy.

## 10. Experience Test

Before adding a page, related-content module, navigation item, or cross-link, ask:

- Does this help a visitor follow a real thread?
- Does it reward curiosity without demanding too much context?
- Does it preserve or clarify something meaningful?
- Does it connect naturally to an existing section?
- Does it avoid turning the site into a funnel, resume, or generic blog?
- Is the next step useful enough to earn the visitor's attention?
- Are privacy, permission, copyright, and attribution handled where relevant?

If the answer is no, the connection should wait, be simplified, or be removed.
