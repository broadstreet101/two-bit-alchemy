# Editorial Workflow

This document describes how raw material becomes publishable Two-Bit Alchemy content.

It is documentation only. It does not modify the website, theme, templates, WordPress configuration, or live content.

## Source Documents

- `docs/creative-constitution.md`
- `docs/voice-and-editorial-guide.md`
- `docs/exhibit-standard.md`
- `content/workbench/README.md`

## 1. Lifecycle

```text
Conversation
      |
      v
Workbench Notes
      |
      v
Editorial Draft
      |
      v
Fact Review
      |
      v
Final Exhibit
      |
      v
Publication
```

## 2. Conversation

Every exhibit begins as conversation rather than polished prose.

Conversation may include:

- A remembered object.
- A project story.
- A half-formed observation.
- A question.
- A photograph or artifact.
- A technical explanation.
- A personal memory that may or may not become public.

At this stage, the goal is capture, not polish. The material can be incomplete, informal, uncertain, or out of order.

## 3. Workbench Notes

The Workbench preserves raw observations.

Workbench notes live under `content/workbench/` and are not considered published. They may contain fragments, questions, lists, placeholders, possible titles, image notes, source notes, or rough chronology.

Workbench rules:

- Capture first.
- Organize lightly.
- Polish later.
- Preserve uncertainty.
- Do not treat Workbench material as public-ready.

The Workbench exists so early material does not disappear just because it is not ready yet.

## 4. Editorial Draft

Editorial work improves structure and clarity without changing facts.

An editorial draft may:

- Organize rough material into sections.
- Clarify the main question or purpose.
- Separate known facts from uncertain memory.
- Identify missing evidence, dates, images, permissions, or context.
- Shape the piece according to the Voice and Editorial Guide.
- Prepare the material for Exhibit Standard review.

Editorial work should not:

- Invent missing details.
- Replace the author's voice.
- Make the story sound more certain than it is.
- Turn personal material into marketing copy.
- Smooth away meaningful roughness or uncertainty.

## 5. Fact Review

Fact Review confirms that the content is honest enough to publish.

Review should check:

- What is known.
- What is remembered but uncertain.
- What needs dates, names, permissions, or source notes.
- Whether images and artifacts have credits or context.
- Whether private or sensitive details should remain unpublished.
- Whether copyright-sensitive material needs review before use.

If a detail cannot be verified, it should be framed honestly or left out.

## 6. Final Exhibit

A Final Exhibit is content that has met the standard for publication.

It should answer the five Exhibit Standard questions:

1. What is this?
2. Why does it matter?
3. What can someone learn from it?
4. Where should they go next?
5. Why does it belong in Two-Bit Alchemy?

A Final Exhibit may still include clearly labeled uncertainty. Completeness is less important than authenticity, but the exhibit should be useful, accessible, and connected to the larger archive.

## 7. Publication

Publication happens only after the exhibit is ready.

Before publication:

- Placeholders should be removed or intentionally retained as public placeholders.
- Related links should point to approved destinations.
- Images should have alt text, captions, credits, and permissions where needed.
- Sensitive material should be reviewed.
- The exhibit should be previewed in context.
- The implementation log should be updated if repository files changed.

Publication does not mean the story can never evolve. It means the current version is honest, useful, and ready to be public.

## 8. Voice Preservation

The author's voice is preserved rather than replaced.

The editorial process should make the writing clearer while keeping it personal, observant, practical, humble, and genuine.

Good editorial support should help the piece sound more like Dada, not less.

Avoid:

- Generic polish.
- Corporate phrasing.
- Over-explaining.
- Exaggerated importance.
- Artificial certainty.
- Removing the human texture that makes the material worth preserving.

## 9. Technical Implementation Boundary

Technical implementation is separate from editorial development.

Theme files, templates, CSS, and WordPress configuration provide structure and presentation. They should not become the only place where personal stories or artifact facts exist.

Editorial material should begin in the repository Workbench or approved content files before it graduates into templates, WordPress content, exhibits, or public pages.

This separation keeps:

- Personal stories editable as content.
- Theme code maintainable.
- Publication decisions reviewable.
- Repository history meaningful.

## 10. Codex Responsibility

Codex supports the workflow but does not author personal stories.

Codex may:

- Create Workbench files.
- Structure notes.
- Preserve placeholders.
- Build templates and content containers.
- Improve clarity without changing facts.
- Identify missing evidence, permissions, or review needs.
- Maintain the implementation log.

Codex should not:

- Invent personal memories.
- Fill emotional or biographical gaps.
- Convert uncertainty into fact.
- Replace the author's voice with generic prose.
- Publish Workbench material as final without approval.

Codex can help build the shelf. The lived story has to come from Dada.

## 11. Editorial Readiness Test

Before moving material forward, ask:

- Has the raw observation been captured?
- Is the source of the memory, fact, image, or artifact clear?
- Are uncertain details labeled honestly?
- Is the author's voice still present?
- Does the draft improve structure without changing facts?
- Does the material meet the Exhibit Standard or clearly remain in Workbench form?
- Is implementation work separate from editorial authorship?

If the answer is no, the material should stay in the Workbench or return to review.
