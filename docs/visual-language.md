# Visual Language

This document defines how imagery is used throughout Two-Bit Alchemy.

It is a planning and editorial reference only. It does not modify the website, implement image treatments, define final artwork placement, or add decorative styling.

## 1. Core Philosophy

Images are evidence, not decoration.

The visual language should make the work easier to understand, remember, inspect, and trust. Images should support observation, process, preservation, and curiosity.

Core rules:

- Authenticity over perfection.
- Documentation over marketing.
- Original material over generic filler.
- Context over polish when context tells the better story.
- Visuals should reward looking closely.

Images should not be added just to make a page feel more designed. If an image does not clarify, preserve, document, or deepen the content, it probably does not belong.

## 2. Photography Principles

Original photography comes first.

Photography should feel close to the work and connected to real experience.

Guidance:

- Use original photographs whenever possible.
- Encourage process photographs, including unfinished work, tools in use, failed attempts, repairs, notes, and experiments.
- Prefer environmental context over isolated product-style shots.
- Preserve imperfections when they tell the story.
- Let lighting, surfaces, wear, age, and mess remain visible when they communicate truth.
- Use composed photographs when helpful, but avoid making the site feel like a catalog or sales page.

Photography should help answer:

- What was being looked at?
- What was happening?
- What changed?
- What mattered?
- What should be remembered?

## 3. Illustration Principles

Illustration should be intentional and content-supportive.

Approved influence:

- Victorian scientific engraving.
- Natural-history illustration.
- Field-journal diagrams.
- Cabinet-of-curiosities references.
- Botanical, celestial, aquatic, and tool-based visual study.

Guidance:

- Use illustration to clarify, frame, or deepen content.
- Use engraving influence with restraint.
- Do not use illustration as filler.
- Do not let illustration dominate the content it is meant to support.
- Avoid fantasy magic, steampunk excess, horror, or purely decorative ornament.

Illustration should feel like it belongs in a field guide, notebook, archive, or study, not like a costume placed over the site.

## 4. Artifact Philosophy

Artifacts are historical material, not decoration.

Possible artifacts:

- Tickets.
- Flyers.
- Field notebooks.
- Handwritten notes.
- Old photographs.
- Sketches.
- Maps.
- Labels.
- Scientific diagrams.

These materials function as evidence of a real thread through time. They can show origin, context, process, influence, memory, place, and decision-making.

Artifact rules:

- Preserve context where possible.
- Include captions when the artifact's meaning is not obvious.
- Keep visible marks, wear, folds, handwriting, and age when they matter.
- Do not over-clean artifacts until their history disappears.
- Do not publish private, sensitive, or copyright-sensitive artifacts without review.
- Do not use artifacts as background texture unless their documentary role has been considered.

Artifacts should help visitors feel that the site preserves real work and real memory.

## 5. Image Hierarchy

### Hero Images

Hero images should be rare and meaningful.

Use when:

- A project, place, object, or photograph is central to the page.
- The image immediately helps visitors understand where they are.
- The image is strong enough to carry first attention without hiding content.

Avoid:

- Generic atmosphere.
- Dark, blurry, cropped, or decorative-only images.
- Hero images that make reading harder.

### Supporting Photographs

Supporting photographs should sit near the relevant text.

Use for:

- Process.
- Materials.
- Before/after comparisons.
- Observed details.
- Contextual evidence.
- Project documentation.

### Galleries

Galleries should support close looking.

Use for:

- Selected photography.
- Project records.
- Cabinet objects.
- Field observations.
- Visual sequences.

Rules:

- Preserve meaningful order.
- Use captions where they add context.
- Avoid dumping large image sets without curation.

### Inline Documentation

Inline images should clarify the text immediately around them.

Use for:

- Steps.
- Examples.
- Observed details.
- Reference comparisons.
- Sketches and diagrams.

Inline documentation should not interrupt reading unless the image is necessary at that point.

### Thumbnails

Thumbnails should help visitors choose where to go next.

Use for:

- Project cards.
- Post cards.
- Gallery previews.
- Cabinet highlights.

Rules:

- Choose thumbnails that identify the content honestly.
- Avoid thumbnails that are visually pretty but misleading.
- Keep crop rules consistent enough for scanning.

## 6. Image Editing Philosophy

Editing should clarify and preserve. It should not fabricate.

Acceptable:

- Color correction.
- Exposure correction.
- Cropping for clarity.
- Dust or scratch cleanup when it does not erase meaningful history.
- Restoration of damaged material when the goal is preservation.
- File optimization for performance.

Avoid:

- Fabricating history.
- Removing meaningful context.
- Making documentary images look falsely pristine.
- Presenting altered or AI-generated imagery as documentary evidence.

AI-generated artwork:

- Must be clearly intentional.
- Must not be presented as documentary evidence.
- Should support approved visual direction.
- Should not replace real photographs, artifacts, or records when those exist.
- Requires review before use in public-facing brand or archival contexts.

## 7. Accessibility

### Alt Text Philosophy

Alt text should communicate the image's purpose.

Rules:

- Informative images need meaningful alt text.
- Decorative images should use empty alt text.
- Artifact images should identify what the artifact is and why it matters when relevant.
- Avoid starting every alt text with "image of" unless needed for clarity.
- Do not stuff alt text with keywords.

Good alt text should help a visitor understand what they would miss if the image were unavailable.

### Captions

Captions should add context.

Use captions for:

- Dates.
- Places.
- Materials.
- Process notes.
- Source context.
- Identification.
- Credit or permission notes.

Captions should not merely repeat visible information.

### Credits

Credits should be preserved when needed.

Rules:

- Credit photographers, artists, sources, archives, or contributors when appropriate.
- Keep credit notes close to the image or in a consistent metadata location.
- Preserve contributor permissions for memorial, family, archive, or submitted materials.

### Licensing

Licensing must be clear before publication.

Rules:

- Do not publish copyrighted archive material without review.
- Do not assume web images are free to use.
- Track source, license, and permission information where relevant.
- Treat future Clutch archive material as copyright-sensitive by default.
- Treat memorial and family materials as permission-sensitive by default.

## 8. Objective Implementation Rules Versus Subjective Artistic Choices

### Objective Implementation Rules

These may be implemented or recommended when they improve clarity, accessibility, performance, or preservation:

- Use real project or documentary images before generic imagery.
- Keep images close to related content.
- Provide meaningful alt text for informative images.
- Use empty alt text for decorative images.
- Add captions when context, source, date, or credit is needed.
- Preserve image credits and licensing notes.
- Optimize images for performance.
- Avoid misleading thumbnails.
- Avoid presenting AI-generated images as evidence.
- Avoid publishing sensitive or copyrighted artifacts without review.

### Subjective Artistic Choices

These require creative approval before implementation:

- Final image crop style.
- Degree of color correction or restoration.
- Gallery density.
- Hero image selection.
- Engraving asset style.
- Illustration density.
- Use of AI-generated artwork.
- How visibly aged or imperfect an artifact should remain.
- Final emblem, favicon, and social preview image treatment.

Decision rule:
If an image choice improves truth, clarity, accessibility, attribution, or performance, it is likely objective. If it primarily changes mood, polish, visual character, or artistic interpretation, treat it as subjective and request approval.
