# Exhibit Standard

This document defines what makes a Two-Bit Alchemy exhibit complete before it is considered ready for publication.

It is a documentation-only standard. It does not modify the theme, templates, CSS, WordPress configuration, or content model.

## 1. Purpose Of An Exhibit

An exhibit is a complete public presentation of a project, object, collection, place, reference, person, experiment, or preserved thread of curiosity.

An exhibit should help visitors understand why something earned a place in Two-Bit Alchemy. It should not merely prove that something exists. It should preserve context, show why it mattered, and offer a path for further wandering.

An exhibit should feel like a carefully labeled drawer in a cabinet: specific, useful, honest, and connected to the rest of the archive.

## 2. Five Core Questions

Every exhibit should answer these questions clearly:

1. What is this?
2. Why does it matter?
3. What can someone learn from it?
4. Where should they go next?
5. Why does it belong in Two-Bit Alchemy?

If an exhibit cannot answer these questions yet, it may remain a draft or placeholder, but it should not be treated as publication-ready.

## 3. Required Narrative Elements

A complete exhibit should include:

- Clear introduction.
- Purpose or reason for preservation.
- Background or origin context.
- What was observed, made, tested, repaired, remembered, or learned.
- Why the material matters beyond surface interest.
- Honest uncertainty where the story is incomplete.
- Relationship to the larger Two-Bit Alchemy archive.
- A useful next step for visitors.

The narrative does not need to be dramatic. It needs to be true enough, specific enough, and useful enough to preserve.

Avoid:

- Resume-like self-framing.
- Generic filler.
- Marketing language.
- Inflated importance.
- Invented certainty.
- Decorative storytelling that hides missing facts.

## 4. Required Structural Elements

A complete exhibit should include:

- One clear page title.
- Introductory orientation text.
- Major sections with meaningful headings.
- Any relevant timeline, process, or chronology when history matters.
- Related Field Notes when durable knowledge exists.
- Related Workshop Journal entries when process or progress exists.
- Related Cabinet items when objects, references, photographs, or artifacts support the exhibit.
- Gallery or image section only when approved images exist.
- Related Projects when relationships are meaningful.
- A restrained "You may also enjoy..." or next-step section when useful.

Do not render empty wrappers as if they are finished content. If content is not ready, use a clearly labeled placeholder or omit the section until it has earned its place.

## 5. Educational Value

An exhibit should teach, preserve, or clarify something.

Educational value may come from:

- A practical lesson.
- A process explanation.
- A documented failure.
- A comparison.
- A method.
- A useful observation.
- A preserved artifact with context.
- A story that helps someone understand a project, person, place, tool, or question.

An exhibit does not need to be instructional in a formal way. It should reward curiosity by making something easier to notice, understand, remember, or follow.

## 6. Related Content Expectations

Related content should be included only when the relationship is real.

Expected relationships:

- Projects connect to Workshop Journal for process.
- Projects connect to Field Notes for durable knowledge.
- Projects connect to Cabinet for artifacts, tools, references, and photographs.
- Workshop Journal entries connect back to Projects when they belong to larger work.
- Workshop Journal entries may connect to Field Notes when a process insight becomes reusable.
- Field Notes connect to Projects, Cabinet items, or Workshop Journal entries when those sources matter.
- Cabinet entries connect to the project, note, or story that gives the object context.

Do not add related links only to make a page look complete.

## 7. Cross-Linking Requirements

Cross-links should help visitors follow a thread.

A publication-ready exhibit should include at least one meaningful next path unless the exhibit is intentionally self-contained.

Cross-links should:

- Use clear destination labels.
- Stay close to the relevant content.
- Support wandering and discovery.
- Avoid overwhelming visitors with too many weak choices.
- Respect the Experience Map's principle of one or two strong next steps over many weak links.

Cross-links should not:

- Exist only for search optimization.
- Interrupt reading without adding meaning.
- Point to unrelated pages.
- Create a funnel-like experience.
- Replace clear navigation or section structure.

## 8. Image Philosophy

Images in exhibits are evidence, not decoration.

A complete exhibit may include images when they:

- Document process.
- Show context.
- Preserve an artifact.
- Clarify a method.
- Reveal a detail.
- Support memory or place.
- Help visitors inspect something that text alone cannot carry.

Image requirements:

- Use original or approved images whenever possible.
- Provide meaningful alt text for informative images.
- Use captions when date, place, source, credit, process, or context matters.
- Preserve credits, permissions, and licensing notes where needed.
- Avoid misleading crops or thumbnails.
- Do not present AI-generated or altered images as documentary evidence.

Do not add images only to make an exhibit feel more designed.

## 9. Evidence Versus Storytelling

Exhibits should balance evidence and storytelling.

Evidence gives trust:

- Photographs.
- Artifacts.
- Notes.
- Measurements.
- Dates.
- Process records.
- Source links.
- Credits.

Storytelling gives meaning:

- Why the thing mattered.
- What changed.
- What was learned.
- What remains unresolved.
- How the exhibit connects to larger curiosity.

Evidence without story can feel like inventory. Story without evidence can feel manufactured. A complete exhibit should use both when both are available.

## 10. Accessibility Expectations

A publication-ready exhibit should be accessible by default.

Minimum expectations:

- One clear `h1`.
- Logical heading order.
- Descriptive link text.
- Meaningful alt text for informative images.
- Empty alt text only for truly decorative images.
- Captions where context, credit, or source is needed.
- Keyboard-accessible links and controls.
- No information conveyed by color alone.
- Text remains readable at normal zoom levels.
- Related content and navigation are labeled clearly.
- Tables, lists, figures, and metadata use appropriate semantic structure.

Accessibility is not a final polish pass. It is part of deciding whether the exhibit is ready.

## 11. Editorial Checklist

Before publication, confirm:

- The exhibit answers the five core questions.
- The voice sounds like Two-Bit Alchemy, not a marketing department.
- The content is authentic and based on real material.
- Unknowns are named honestly.
- No personal facts have been invented.
- No project history has been retroactively polished into something false.
- The exhibit teaches, preserves, or clarifies something.
- The introduction is specific and not filler.
- The purpose is clear.
- The visitor has a meaningful next step.
- The exhibit belongs in an existing section or has a documented reason to require a new one.
- The content would still feel worth preserving years from now.

If an exhibit fails several of these checks, keep it in draft form.

## 12. Publication Checklist

Before marking an exhibit ready for publication, confirm:

- Final content has been approved.
- Placeholders have been removed or intentionally retained as clearly labeled public placeholders.
- Related links point to existing or approved destinations.
- Images, captions, alt text, credits, and permissions are complete.
- Copyright-sensitive material has been reviewed.
- Privacy-sensitive material has been reviewed.
- Navigation path is clear from the relevant parent section.
- "You may also enjoy..." links are meaningful and limited.
- Page structure matches the Component Library and Layout System principles.
- The exhibit has been previewed on desktop and mobile.
- Keyboard navigation and focus states have been checked.
- The implementation log has been updated if repository files changed.

Publication means the exhibit is honest, useful, accessible, and connected. It does not need to be exhaustive.
