# Creative Constitution

This document defines the long-term creative purpose of Two-Bit Alchemy.

It is not marketing copy. It is not an About page. It is an internal reference used to evaluate future ideas, content, design decisions, and implementation priorities.

## 1. Purpose

Two-Bit Alchemy exists to collect, preserve, and share the things that reward curiosity.

The site should document genuine curiosity, real projects, observations, discoveries, artifacts, and experiments. Its common thread is not a list of hobbies. Its common thread is learning enough about something to make something meaningful with it.

Two-Bit Alchemy should help preserve what was noticed, made, tested, learned, repaired, photographed, remembered, or understood.

## 2. Philosophy

Two-Bit Alchemy is built around the joy of learning by making.

Its creative center is:

- Observe.
- Record.
- Understand.
- Create.

The site should feel like being invited into a thoughtful workshop rather than reading someone's private journal. It can be personal, reflective, and specific, but it should also give visitors something to examine, follow, learn from, or return to.

The work should feel grounded in craft, science, memory, and close attention. Curiosity should lead the structure. Technology, design, and documentation should support that curiosity quietly.

## 3. What Belongs Here

Content belongs on Two-Bit Alchemy when it preserves or shares real curiosity.

Belongs here:

- Real projects and their histories.
- Field observations and durable notes.
- Workshop process, experiments, repairs, failures, and lessons.
- Photographs that document or reveal something.
- Cabinet objects, tools, books, specimens, artifacts, and references.
- Fisher Aquatics as an important project history within the broader identity.
- Personal context that helps visitors understand the work without becoming resume-like.
- Future memorial material when handled with care, permission, and respect.
- Future archive projects when copyright, attribution, and preservation boundaries are clear.

Authenticity is more important than completeness. A small true record is better than a polished but manufactured archive.

## 4. What Does Not Belong Here

Content does not belong here merely because it fills space, follows a trend, or makes the site look more complete.

Avoid:

- Manufactured content created only to make the site feel larger.
- Generic blog posts without a real observation, project, artifact, or question behind them.
- Sales-first positioning.
- Corporate language.
- Resume-like personal framing.
- Treating Fisher Aquatics as the whole identity.
- Decorative content that does not support the story.
- Private material that has not been intentionally prepared for public context.
- Copyright-sensitive archive material without review.
- Reactive changes that overwrite approved direction without a clear reason.

## 5. Visitor Experience

The site should encourage wandering and discovery rather than simply searching.

Visitors should feel they are moving through connected rooms in a cabinet of curiosities:

- Projects show bodies of work.
- Field Notes preserve durable knowledge.
- Workshop Journal shows process and current curiosity.
- Cabinet gathers objects, references, photographs, and artifacts.
- About gives enough personal context to orient the visitor.
- Contact stays available without turning the site into a sales funnel.

Each major section should feel distinct but related. The visitor should be able to follow a thread from a project to a note, from a note to an artifact, from an artifact to a photograph, and from a photograph back to a story.

The experience should reward close attention without requiring visitors to already understand the whole archive.

## 6. Creative Principles

Creative work should follow these principles:

- Preserve real stories, projects, and observations rather than manufacturing content.
- Show process, not just finished outcomes.
- Let photographs and artifacts support the story.
- Keep the tone personal, clear, warm, observant, and patient.
- Favor specificity over polish when both cannot be achieved at once.
- Let the site feel handmade without making it fragile or hard to use.
- Use design to clarify and invite, not to perform.
- Treat Victorian natural-history engraving as an approved visual language, not as decoration to apply everywhere.
- Keep animation minimal and purposeful.
- Let future ideas earn their place through repeated need, not novelty.

## 7. Preservation Principles

Two-Bit Alchemy should preserve the history of ideas instead of rewriting it.

Preservation means:

- Keep useful project history visible.
- Record decisions and reasons.
- Maintain the implementation log.
- Preserve source material, credits, and context where needed.
- Prefer real chronology over retroactive perfection.
- Keep legacy identities, such as Fisher Aquatics, understandable inside the broader archive.
- Protect private or sensitive material until it is intentionally prepared for publication.
- Review copyright and attribution before publishing archive material.

The repository and documentation should continue to evolve deliberately rather than reactively. Major creative, structural, or technical changes should be documented before they become permanent implementation.

## 8. Decision Test

Use this checklist before adding a future feature, content area, design element, or implementation change.

Ask:

- Does this reward curiosity?
- Is it based on a real project, observation, artifact, story, question, or need?
- Does it help someone understand, follow, preserve, or explore something meaningful?
- Does it belong in one of the existing rooms of the site before creating a new one?
- Does it support wandering and discovery without making navigation confusing?
- Does it preserve authenticity over completeness?
- Does it avoid turning the site into a corporate pitch, resume, trend piece, or generic blog?
- Are privacy, permissions, copyright, and attribution handled where relevant?
- Can it be maintained in the repository, documentation, WordPress content model, or editorial workflow without becoming fragile?
- If it changes an approved direction, has the reason been documented and approved?

If the answer to several of these questions is no, the idea should wait, be simplified, or be discarded.
