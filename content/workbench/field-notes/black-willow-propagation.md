# Black Willow Propagation

## Observation

- Black willow cuttings were originally obtained by mistake after ordering weeping willow.
- The trees have been grown for several years.
- Experiments progressed naturally:
  - growing seedlings
  - rooting cuttings
  - simple bonsai pruning
- Water used to root black willow cuttings appears to dramatically improve rooting success in other plants.
- Lavender successfully rooted in the same water despite being an unexpected candidate.
- This is practical observation, not scientific proof or controlled experimentation.

## What Happened

- Black willow cuttings were rooted in water.
- The same water was later used with other plant cuttings.
- Other plants appeared to root more successfully in that water.
- Lavender rooted successfully in the same water.
- Lavender was an unexpected rooting candidate in this context.

## Working Explanation

- Heidi O'Toole explained that black willow contains natural rooting compounds.
- Heidi is the source of the practical insight connecting the observed rooting success to naturally occurring rooting compounds in black willow.
- This explanation created the "head-slapping" moment where years of observation finally made sense.
- This remains a working explanation based on practical observation.
- No controlled experiment has been recorded yet.

## Practical Takeaway

- Water used for rooting black willow cuttings may be useful when attempting to root other plant cuttings.
- This should be treated as a practical technique to observe further, not a proven universal method.
- Future use should record plant species, timing, water age, cutting condition, and success rate.
